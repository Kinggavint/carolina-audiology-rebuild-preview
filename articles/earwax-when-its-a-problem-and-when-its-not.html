Earwax: When It Is a Problem and When It Is Not

By the team at Carolina Audiology Associates

Earwax has a public relations problem. Most people think of it as gross. In reality, earwax is one of the body's most elegant defense systems. It traps debris, keeps the ear canal moisturized, and has natural antimicrobial properties. You want some of it.

When earwax becomes a problem

Earwax becomes a problem when it builds up faster than it sheds, when it gets pushed deeper by a cotton swab, or when ear canal anatomy traps it in place. Signs of a blockage include:

  • Muffled hearing on one or both sides
  • A sense of fullness or pressure in the ear
  • Itching or mild discomfort
  • Tinnitus that appears suddenly
  • Hearing aids that suddenly sound weak or dead

Why cotton swabs make it worse

Cotton swabs push wax deeper into the canal, where it compacts against the eardrum. The labels on the swabs themselves tell you not to use them in the ear canal. The body sheds wax on its own. The outer ear is the only place a swab belongs.

What about ear candles and drops

Ear candling does not work and can cause burns and canal injury. Over-the-counter softening drops can help loosen mild buildup but do not remove impacted wax. They also are not safe for everyone, particularly people with a history of eardrum perforation or tubes.

Professional earwax removal

In our office, we remove earwax under direct visualization, using techniques chosen for the health and comfort of your ear canal. Most appointments take 15 to 20 minutes, and many patients are surprised by how much clearer everything sounds the moment the blockage is gone.

If your hearing has dropped suddenly or you can feel fullness in your ear, book a wax removal appointment. It is one of the simplest and most satisfying visits we do.
